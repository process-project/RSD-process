Encouraging the re-use of research software

Much of modern day science would not be possible without the use of computers, and as a result, many scientists spend a significant part of their time writing code. During the past 5 years or so since the Netherlands eScience Center first started working with scientists, some patterns related to research software have emerged.

For example, research software often contains components that are quite generic in their application. To give you some idea, this may include components to draw certain types of linked plots, or to connect to a cluster computer located elsewhere, or to let a computer program take some of its inputs from the command line. Indeed, one may ask why such components are being developed in research programs?

On finding software

Apart from technical reasons relating to engineering quality of research software, the most important reason is that it is difficult to successfully search for software. Scientists, like most people these days, depend on search engines whenever they are looking for information on something. However, when looking for software, that approach is fraught with confusion and frustration. Sure, if you know the name of something, Googling it is easy enough, but what if you don't know what the right keywords are? For the examples above, most researchers probably don't know to search for trellis plots and brushing when looking for linked plots. Similarly, they would not know that the type of component used to connect to a cluster goes by the ill-chosen name of middleware, and that a computer program can read its inputs from the command line by way of an argument parser.

Search engines do not 'understand' software repositories like GitHub, SourceForge, or BitBucket

Even if you happen to know what keywords to search for, the sites listed in the search results typically do not answer questions like "What can this tool do for me?", "Where do I go to get started?", "Are other people in my field using it and what are they using it for?"'

Visible impact

Funders want to steer on creating the most impact, but they have very little information to go on. The Research Software Directory makes transparent what the impact of software is.
